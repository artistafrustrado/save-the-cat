3 - Set-up Pages [Pages 1 - 10]

Use your first ten pages wisely. Here, you'll need to establish your story scope (and "look and feel") for the audience.

First, show your character in their "Old World". Let the audience know what the status quo is for them, then hint at the adventure that follows.

Second, establish all characters who will factor into your main story beats. This make take some creativity; Sometimes, for story reasons, you will just have to hint at those Characters.

The setup is one of the most important sections of your scripts because it provides the essencial context needed for the audience to become either immediatly engaged, or lose interest within the first ten minutes. Obriousley, as a screenwriter, the former is required to sustain a carrer.

The Matrix begins with an action sequence cleverly to establish how there are people on the workd (Trinity, The Agents), who seem like they are from another place all toghether.

They defy the laws of physics, and seem go come and go as they please.

We also meet Neo, and learn that he senses this other world, but it is just beyonf his grasp. When you want your audience to get engaged quickly, it's these places of essencial context that will make that happen.

From here on out, your Save The Cat beat sheet template will be filled with obstacles and twists resulting from making this choice.

9 - Midpoint [Page 55]

At some point, the protagonist will either get what they are after... or not. But there will be consequences either way. Neo takes the red pill to see if really he's truly humanity's savior. But, at the midpoint, he goes to the clairvoyant Oracle to see if this is true.

She gives him a once-over and, tragically, tells him it's not true.

10 - Bad Guys Close In [Pages 55-75]

After your protagonist gets what they want, or not, there will be consequences. Those forces will tighten their grasp, and throw the protagonist off balance. Those forces don't necessarily have to be actual antagonists. It can also be fighting within the protagonist's circle.

Back to The Matrix. this is where Cypher betrays the team and allows the Agents to capture Morpheus. This thwarts the teams's relative control among chaos.

11 - All Is Lost

In film structure, the dire circunstances your protagonist endures will lead to an inevitable loss. This is usually a character, and classically the "mentor" (think Obi-Wan, Gandalf and Morpheus).

Whatever the loss, it's felt deeply because this item (or person) gave the protagonist their bearings. In The Matrix, this is where Morpheus is kidnapped, and Agent Smith presses him to give up the location of Zion - Humanity's safe haven.

Executing decisions like these might be challenging, but definettly necessary to create the best script. If you are writing with a team, or a partner, it might be even harder. Not everyone will agree, so it's a must to stay on the same page, along the way.
